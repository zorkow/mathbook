Proof

By DeMoivre's Theorem,

\begin{equation*} z^n = \cis \left( n \frac{2 k \pi}{n } \right) = \cis( 2 k \pi ) = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}

The \(z\)'s are distinct since the numbers \(2 k \pi /n\) are all distinct and are greater than or equal to 0 but less than \(2 \pi\text{.}\) The fact that these are all of the roots of the equation \(z^n=1\) follows from from CorollaryÂ 17.9, which states that a polynomial of degree \(n\) can have at most \(n\) roots. We will leave the proof that the \(n\)th roots of unity form a cyclic subgroup of \({\mathbb T}\) as an exercise.
