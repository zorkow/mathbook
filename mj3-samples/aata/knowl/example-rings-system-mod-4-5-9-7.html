Example 16.44.

Let us solve the system

\begin{align*} x & \equiv 3 \pmod{4}\\ x & \equiv 4 \pmod{5}\\ x & \equiv 1 \pmod{9}\\ x & \equiv 5 \pmod{7}\text{.} \end{align*}

From ExampleÂ 16.42 we know that \(19\) is a solution of the first two congruences and any other solution of the system is congruent to \(19 \pmod{20}\text{.}\) Hence, we can reduce the system to a system of three congruences:

\begin{align*} x & \equiv 19 \pmod{20}\\ x & \equiv 1 \pmod{9}\\ x & \equiv 5 \pmod{7}\text{.} \end{align*}

Solving the next two equations, we can reduce the system to

\begin{align*} x & \equiv 19 \pmod{180}\\ x & \equiv 5 \pmod{7}\text{.} \end{align*}

Solving this last system, we find that \(19\) is a solution for the system that is unique up to modulo \(1260\text{.}\)
